Unix Operating System
Initial Release: 1969 at Bell Labs
Developers: Ken Thompson, Dennis Ritchie, Brian Kernighan, Douglas McIlroy and team.
Languages Used: C, Assembly
Official Website: unix.org

Introduction

Unix is a computer operating system developed in 1969 at Bell Labs by Ken Thompson, Dennis Ritchie, Douglas McIlroy, and others. Originally written in assembly, it was rewritten in C in 1973, enabling portability across hardware platforms.


History

Unix emerged after the Multics project at Bell Labs, where core contributors sought a simpler system. Version 6 Unix was sold to universities, influencing the creation of BSD. System V and BSD variants led to widespread adoption and eventual fragmentation into multiple commercial and research versions.


Key Features

  1. Modular kernel with multitasking and multiuser support.
  2. Hierarchical filesystem with “everything is a file” model.
  3. Introduced pipes and redirection via the Thompson shell (the first Unix shell, written by Ken Thompson)
  4. Portable across hardware due to rewrite in the C programming language
  5. The Unix philosophy: simple, modular tools and composability

Influence & Legacy

Unix's design shaped countless operating systems. Its open licensing to universities led to BSD development; Unix-like systems include FreeBSD, OpenBSD, Solaris, AIX, Darwin, and Linux. Today Unix based operating system rule the computer world.
